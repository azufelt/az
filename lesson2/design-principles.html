Repetition

Joss & Main
www.jossandmain.com

The Joss & Main site is a perfect example of repetition! Instantly upon arriving at their mobile web homepage, two ideas of repetition are encountered.

The first is the repetitive long, horizontal, shades-of-black banner bars that stretch across the screen view. Each of these bars lists a different navigation function for the user, but the options have been simplified by unifying their visual qualities.

Further down the scroll, the user will find a plethora of navigation options. Each option is listed in an orderly fashion, paired with a a simplified icon representing the correlating shopping department. â€”Much like a shopper would find within a physical store shopping experience.

Instead of becoming overwhelmed by choices, the user can quickly decide which department they are searching for and within seconds are able to navigate accordingly.

Proximity

Pottery Barn
www.potterybarn.com

Pottery Barn utilizes the Law of Proximity on their mobile web homepage. It is further supported by the Law of Similarity.

The designer has grouped similar items close to one another, as well as in the same colored box. For instance, the top gray banner is multiple brands that are branches of the main company, so if a user wanted to navigate easily to them, it will be easy to decipher that they are all of the company's other brands.

Moving down the homepage we can see that the white box has many different black-lined icons grouped. Users have come to expect that these icons will be found close to one another (search tool, favorite items, hamburger drop-down menu).

Followed immediately by a black banner box that groups together three ideas that revolve around shipping. And then of course our main visual box is comprised of a background image showcasing their newest product, and a black button that takes you directly to their shopping.

Each of the sections show that the site designers planned accordingly to have like items share proximal real estate, simplifying the users' experience.

Negative Space

MAGNOLIA
www.magnolia.com

Interacting with the MAGNOLIA website on a mobile device couldn't be simpler or more refreshing! It makes great use of negative space throughout the site but doesn't limit it to being just plain white.

The first image that appears on the homepage even reinforces a pleasing amount of negative space. Because of the generous use of negative space, our eyes are at rest and we are directed exactly where to go: the "SHOP NOW" button that appears above the fold, in bold black.

By keeping the surrounding background free of any visual elements, other than the title, the viewer does not have to wonder where to navigate next, or where they should start shopping.

As the user maneuvers through this site on a mobile device, their finger will naturally fall on the over-sized "SHOP NOW" button, which sports a generous amount of padding reinforcing the simplicity of their shopping experience.
